How to Create an ATS-Friendly Resume in 2026

Updated for 2026 · Career Craft Blog

In 2026, the hiring process is still heavily filtered by Applicant Tracking Systems, commonly known as ATS. These systems scan resumes before a recruiter or hiring manager sees them. That means your resume must be readable by software, not just visually attractive to humans. A strong design can help a resume stand out, but if the ATS cannot parse it correctly, the document may never reach the next stage. The good news is that making a resume ATS-friendly is not difficult. It mostly requires clarity, consistency, and the right structure.

The first rule is to keep your resume simple. Avoid tables, text boxes, images, charts, and overly decorative layouts. Many ATS tools struggle with complex formatting and may mix up sections or skip important details. Instead, use a clean layout with standard headings such as Professional Summary, Work Experience, Education, Skills, and Certifications. These headings help both the ATS and the recruiter quickly understand your background. The simpler the structure, the safer your resume becomes for automated screening.

Next, focus on keywords. ATS software scans your resume for terms that match the job description. If a company is hiring for a Laravel Developer, the job post may mention PHP, Laravel, MySQL, REST APIs, Git, and deployment workflows. Those exact terms should appear naturally in your resume if they match your actual experience. Do not stuff random keywords into your CV. The goal is to reflect your real skills in a way that mirrors the language used by employers. A well-optimized resume balances natural writing with role-specific terms.

Formatting also matters. Use one professional font like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman. Keep font sizes readable, usually 10 to 12 points for body text and a little larger for headings. Maintain consistent spacing, left alignment, and clear section breaks. Recruiters often skim resumes quickly, so readability is just as important as ATS compatibility. A cluttered document can reduce your chances even if the content is strong. Short bullet points work better than long paragraphs because they are easier to scan.

Your work experience section should highlight achievements rather than only duties. For example, instead of writing “Responsible for website maintenance,” say “Improved website performance by 35% through code optimization and caching improvements.” Numbers make your impact more convincing and help your resume look stronger. ATS systems do not “care” about style, but humans do. When the ATS passes your resume along, a recruiter should immediately see measurable value.

Another important tip is to tailor each resume to the job you are applying for. A generic CV often performs poorly because different roles require different skills and keywords. If you are applying to a frontend role, emphasize JavaScript, React, UI implementation, and performance optimization. If you are applying to a backend role, focus on APIs, databases, server-side logic, and testing. Small adjustments can make a big difference in whether your application gets shortlisted.

Saving your file in the right format is also essential. PDF is usually the safest choice because it preserves layout and is easy to read across devices. However, if the employer specifically requests a DOCX file, follow that instruction. Always make sure your exported file opens correctly and that the text remains selectable. A broken file, unreadable formatting, or missing text can immediately hurt your chances.

Finally, proofread carefully before submitting. Even a good ATS-friendly resume can fail if it contains spelling mistakes, inconsistent dates, or missing contact information. Check your email, phone number, LinkedIn profile, and portfolio links. If you are using a resume builder, review the preview before downloading. A polished resume signals professionalism, attention to detail, and readiness for the job market.
